// 05 — ABOUT

From pharmacy to pipelines

I studied pharmacy at Hanoi University of Pharmacy — and from my first year, I worked. Sales, medical rep, marketing, medical affairs: I tried nearly every role the industry offered, hoping each one would be "the one". I could do the work, but I never caught myself opening the laptop after hours just out of curiosity. That, I realized, was the real test.

The turn came in medical affairs. A mentor pointed at a chart in a paper and asked: "Work out how they computed this." I spent days re-reading the methodology, re-deriving every number — and loved it. Not solving the puzzle, but watching scattered numbers become logic. Later, marketing showed me the business side: campaigns that looked loud but didn't convert, quiet ones that did. Data didn't make the decisions — it made them less emotional.

So I learned, one real problem at a time: Python when Excel stopped scaling, SQL when I got tired of waiting for someone else to pull data, Power BI when analyses needed an audience — then dbt, warehousing and modeling when I noticed the hard part was never the dashboard, but everything underneath it. I've never learned a tool because it was hot; only because a problem demanded it.

Today, as an analytics engineer, my job in one sentence: help the business trust its data before using it to decide. That means sitting with teams to agree on what a "new customer" even is, then encoding that agreement into models, tests and a semantic layer — so meetings stop debating whose report is right and start debating what to do next.

Pharmacy didn't teach me SQL. It taught me to ask how a number was made before accepting it, and that one wrong assumption changes every conclusion downstream. I no longer call what I did a career switch — I carried one field's discipline into another field's problems.

"Don't just learn how to find the answer — learn to understand why that answer exists."

Outside work I help run Xóm Data, Vietnam's data community of 100,000+ members, and teach — because explaining something is the fastest way to find out what you don't actually understand yet.
