01 / THE ANSWER

The data supports a focused validation phase, not a citywide launch.

NYC trip activity grew across the analysis window, but growth sits inside a market dominated by two platforms. Activity is dense in Manhattan, while several Queens zones combine growth, fare, and airport relevance. Friday and Saturday evenings show the clearest recurring operating window.

My recommendation: investigate a small number of Queens–Manhattan and airport-oriented scenarios, then test driver supply, charging, customer acquisition, regulation, and unit economics before committing fleet capacity.

PROCEED WITH

Corridor-level discovery

DO NOT CLAIM

Unmet demand or profitability

VALIDATE NEXT

Supply, charging, acquisition cost, regulation

03 / DECISION CONTEXT

Completed trips show activity. They do not show the whole market.

A market-entry team needs more than a citywide trip count: competitive concentration, geographic density, time windows, fare position, and the evidence gaps that could change a fleet decision.

TLC data records completed trips. It does not capture rejected requests, unserved passengers, online drivers, idle vehicles, or charging availability. I treated that boundary as part of the analysis rather than a footnote.

SO WHAT? A new entrant cannot assume market growth translates into attainable share. Customer acquisition, driver liquidity, reliability, and differentiation need their own evidence.

06 / OPERATING PATTERNS

Friday and Saturday evenings provide the clearest test window.

The largest zone-hour aggregates recur between 18:00 and 23:00 on Friday and Saturday — supporting a controlled evening-shift experiment with defined geography and service level. It does not determine fleet size: vehicle requirements still depend on supply, turnaround, charging, deadhead miles and utilization targets.

07 / RECOMMENDATION

Move to the next evidence gate before moving vehicles.

Advance to a narrow validation stage. Compare a Long Island City–Manhattan corridor with a LaGuardia-oriented scenario. Define the operating window, service-level target, and decision thresholds before any pilot.

1

Compare LIC–Manhattan and LaGuardia-oriented scenarios.

2

3

10 / WHAT THE DATA CANNOT ANSWER

The missing data determines whether the opportunity is operable.

Requests, cancellations, ETA

→ separate completed activity from unmet demand

Driver online & idle time

→ estimate supply coverage and utilization

Charging locations & dwell time

→ test EV feasibility by zone and shift

Weather, events, transit disruption

→ explain short-term variation

Insurance, labor, acquisition, energy cost

→ build contribution-margin scenarios

Customer research & incentives

→ test willingness to switch and acquisition cost

Good analysis does not remove uncertainty. It makes the next uncertainty explicit and testable.

11 / REFLECTION — WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY

What this project changed in my analytical practice.

The hardest part was not processing 843.8 million rows. It was keeping the decision claim smaller than the dataset.

I learned to separate observed activity from demand, place metric definitions upstream of the dashboard, expose the assumptions behind a score, and connect every finding to a decision or a missing piece of evidence. The result is both an analytics engineering project and a data analysis case study: reliable enough to inspect, clear enough to question, and honest about what it cannot prove.
